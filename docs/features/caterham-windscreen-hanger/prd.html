Caterham Windscreen Hanger — PRD

A pair of printed brackets that store a removed Caterham Seven windscreen on the garage wall — hung by its steel stanchions, so nothing ever bears on the glass.

00

The problem & the shape of the fix

Problem

Pull the windscreen for a track day and you're holding a ~5 kg slab of curved laminated glass in an alloy frame, stanchions still attached. On the floor it's underfoot and easy to scuff; leaning it loads the glass and frame; commercial racks barely exist and a timber one is bulky and still bears on the wrong parts.

Solution

Two identical printed brackets carry the screen by its stanchions — the steel parts built to take its weight. It hangs inverted and flat to the wall. The cutouts face perpendicular to the wall, so each stanchion juts straight out and the bracket turns 90° — out, then a sideways wedge through the cutout, which nests on and self-centres. Each braced arm carries ~2.5 kg as a short cantilever; the wall reacts the lean. One non-handed part, printed twice, mounted at any spacing.

User stories

Everything the owner should be able to do — one actor: the Caterham owner.

Store my removed windscreen off the garage floor, so it isn't underfoot or at risk of being kicked.
Have the mount carry the screen by its steel stanchions, so no load rests on the glass or alloy frame.
Hang the screen flat against the wall and off the floor, so it's safely stored and the wall reacts the tipping load.
Have each triangular cutout nest onto a wedge, so the screen self-centres and can't slide sideways.

03

Implementation decisions

Settled during the grill — see ADR-0001.

Load path & orientation — per ADR-0001

Hang the windscreen inverted and flat to the wall from the two stanchion triangular cutouts. All load through steel; nothing on glass or frame.

One symmetric, non-handed bracket · printed ×2

No mirrored variant. The two brackets are independent, so the owner sets any spacing to match their stanchions and studs. Symmetry about the vertical mid-plane is what makes it non-handed.

Engagement — 90° turn to a nesting wedge

The cutout faces perpendicular to the wall, so the bracket turns 90°: an arm out from the wall, then a sideways wedge through the cutout. A downward-pointing isosceles wedge matches the ~76×76 mm cutout, undersized by a nesting clearance so the steel seats on the faces and self-centres — load along two faces, not one edge. Wedges point inward.

Retention — tilt and ridge

Wedge tilted up ~5–10° so the screen settles into it, plus a small ridge along the outer top of the arm, so an upward knock can't lift the screen off.

Wall plate — spread fixing

Flat plate with a spread, countersunk hole pattern: one hole to catch a stud where it falls, plus outer holes for toggle/butterfly anchors in bare plasterboard. ~2.5 kg/bracket sits well inside anchor ratings.

Structure — short braced cantilever, PETG

The wedge sits ~38 mm (1.5″) out from the wall, so each arm carries ~2.5 kg as a short cantilever — under 1 N·m, a small moment with a big PETG margin. A modest gusset at the 90° corner; print oriented so bending can't peel layers there. No supports.

Parametric interface

Top-level parameters: cutout base & height, plate thickness, wedge nesting clearance, wedge tilt, ridge size, arm reach, wall-plate dimensions, and the screw/anchor pattern — so a re-measure is a quick edit.

Deliverables — repo product-folder convention

Parametric source, an STL export, a render.conf sidecar (Catppuccin Mocha, ¾ view, bold edges) driving the shared render.sh, a render.png, and a README with BOM / mount / print notes under CC BY-NC 4.0. Added to the root index.

Deferred to design time
Exact wedge dimensions against the measured cutout · exact plate thickness · exact screw/anchor hole spacing.

Module sketch & verification

One deep module behind a small parametric interface.

Deep module

Windscreen Hanger bracket

One symmetric bracket: wall plate + a 90° arm to an up-tilted sideways wedge + retaining ridge + spread anchor pattern — all driven by a handful of top-level parameters. Lots of geometry, a narrow interface.

  • Wall plate — flat mounting face, countersunk spread holes
  • 90° arm + wedge — reach out, turn, nesting sideways wedge
  • Retaining ridge — outer-top lip against lift-off
Verification — visual + print-fit

No automated tests, consistent with every product in this repo. The parametric interface is the durable seam: a bad fit is a parameter edit, not a geometry rewrite.

  • Visual — a clean CGAL render (also proves it's manifold & printable) plus the Mocha ¾ render for eyeball review
  • Print-fit — print one, confirm the cutout nests, self-centres & the retention holds before printing the second
  • Bounding box — confirmed within a ~256 mm bed
05

Out of scope & notes

Out of scope
  • Displaying the screen glass-out / right-way-up — it hangs inverted, glass to wall
  • Storing the aeroscreen, or a screen with stanchions removed
  • A single full-width rack spanning both stanchions
  • Supplying screws, anchors, or protective felt
  • Any change to the shared render.sh
Further notes
  • Glass faces the wall — protected, not displayed; adhesive felt on the wedge faces is an optional owner add
  • Protrusion — the stanchions point straight out, so the screen stands off the wall by their ~38 mm projection
  • Mirror clearance — a mirror left on may near the wall when inverted; check, or remove first
  • Two-hand job — inverting & hanging 5 kg of glass is a deliberate careful lift
