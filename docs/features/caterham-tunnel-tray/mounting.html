Mounting Deep Dive

How does it stay on the tunnel?

Five mounting strategies, ranked. The right answer depends on how your S3's tunnel cover is shaped, what's under the dash, and how reversible you want it to be.

00 The core challenge

The tray lives in a high-vibration, high-temperature environment and needs to survive aggressive cornering without letting go. Here's what the mounting system must resist:

1. Shear (lateral)

Cornering forces push the tray sideways. On a 420R with sticky tyres, that's roughly 0.9–1.1 G lateral acceleration.

A 500 g loaded tray experiences ~5 N of shear force. This is actually the easy direction for adhesives.

2. Peel (lift-off)

The worst-case load. Someone bumps the tray with their knee, or you grab it to pull yourself out of the seat. Adhesive joints fail catastrophically in peel.

This is the failure mode every adhesive-only mount suffers.

3. Heat soak

The transmission tunnel downstream of a 2.0L Duratec can reach 60–80 °C on a hot day after a hard drive. Adhesives creep and soften well below their rated max.

3M Dual Lock is rated to ~93 °C continuous, but peel strength drops significantly above 50 °C.

01 Five strategies, ranked

Recommended

🥇 Dash-captured + Dual Lock

The tray has a thin front tongue that slides under the dashboard. The dash overhang mechanically prevents the tray from lifting. Dual Lock patches on the tunnel floor prevent fore-aft / lateral sliding.

  • Peel immunity — the dash is a mechanical stop. Adhesive never sees peel loads.
  • Shear handled by Dual Lock — its strongest axis.
  • Fully reversible — no drilling, no adhesive failure over time.
  • Removal: slide the tray rearward (out from under the dash), then lift.
  • Requires ~10–15 mm of accessible gap under the dash at the tunnel centreline.
Side section · dash-captured
🥈 Tunnel-edge clamp

The tray has downward flanges that hook around the sides of the tunnel cover, clamping the edges. No adhesive at all.

  • Pure mechanical — zero adhesive, zero creep.
  • Reversible. Spring-loaded or screw-tightened clamp.
  • Requires the tunnel side flanges to be exposed and accessible.
  • The tunnel might be carpeted or have sill trim overlapping the edge.
  • Clamp could mar the tunnel edge finish.
No adhesive Needs exposed flanges
🥉 Full-surface VHB

3M VHB 4950 tape across the entire tray floor. Permanent bond.

  • Very strong in shear — the full footprint works for you.
  • Essentially permanent — removal risks gel-coat delamination.
  • Still vulnerable to peel at the edges when bumped.
  • Heat reduces bond strength over multiple cycles.
  • No way to remove the tray for cleaning or access.
Permanent Strong shear
Magnets

Embed neodymium magnets in the tray floor, with a steel plate or matching magnets bonded to the tunnel underside.

  • Tool-free removal — just lift.
  • Needs access to the tunnel underside to place the steel plate.
  • Magnet strength vs thickness tradeoff — strong magnets are thick.
  • Can interfere with phones / credit cards in the tray.
  • Vibration can cause magnets to walk or shift over time.
Tool-free Needs underside access
Shared screw tabs

The tunnel cover is held on by screws along its sides. The tray has tabs that slot under the same screws.

  • Rock solid — borrowing the car's own fastening system.
  • Only works if the tunnel-side screws are accessible from inside the cockpit.
  • Tray needs to align with existing screw positions (spacing varies).
  • Requires slightly longer screws. Totally reversible.
  • Best for a permanent / semi-permanent install.
Very secure Needs screw alignment

03 The dash-captured approach in detail

Here's why I think this is the right answer — and how the design works.

How the mechanics work

Lifting prevented by the dash

The tray's front edge has a thin (wall-thick) tongue that extends ~15 mm forward. This slides into the gap between the tunnel top and the underside of the dashboard. The dash sits above it — the tray physically cannot lift up. Knee bumps, grabbing the tray, even aggressive cornering Gs — none of it can peel the tray off.

Sliding prevented by Dual Lock

Three 25×25 mm patches of 3M Dual Lock (SJ3550) distributed along the tray floor. In pure shear, each patch holds ~7 kg. Three patches = over 20 kg of shear capacity — more than enough for a phone + keys + wallet. The Dual Lock never sees peel loads because the dash stops lifting.

Thermal isolation

The tray floor sits directly on the tunnel, so it will get warm. But ASA handles 80 °C comfortably. A ~2 mm air gap under the lid provides some convection cooling. If the tunnel is notably hot, you could add a thin cork or silicone gasket under the tray.

You need to check
  • Is there ~12 mm of vertical gap between the tunnel top and the lowest part of the dash, at the centreline?
  • How far forward does the dash overhang? The tongue needs ~15 mm of insertion. Is there space?
  • Are there wires / heater cables hanging under the dash in that area? They might need a small routing clip to stay clear.
  • Is the tunnel surface smooth? Dual Lock needs a clean, flat surface to bond to. Painted aluminium is ideal. Textured gel coat may need a light sand.
Quick test
Cut a piece of 2 mm cardboard to the planned tray shape, slide it forward under the dash. Does it fit? Does it stay when you let go? If yes — the dash capture works.

04 Plan B: If the dash doesn't cooperate

Maybe the dash gap is too tight, or wires block it. Here's the fallback.

Tunnel-edge wrap clamp

The tray has integral C-channel flanges on both sides that reach down and grip the tunnel cover's side edges. A single M4 screw per side (captive nut in the flange) tightens the clamp. No adhesive, no dash required.

  • Requires the tunnel cover's side edges to be exposed
  • Adds ~15 mm of width per side for the clamp flange
  • The flanges sit below the tray floor — they don't widen the tray itself
  • Can be padded with thin felt to avoid marking the tunnel
  • Removable in seconds with a screwdriver
Pure mechanical No dash needed

05 So — what's under your dash?

Two questions to answer

1. Can you slide a tongue under the dash?

Have a look under the dash at the tunnel centreline. Is there a gap between the tunnel top and the dash panel's lower edge? If there's even ~10 mm of space, the dash-capture approach works.

2. Are the tunnel edges accessible?

If the dash gap is blocked, look at the tunnel sides. Can you see the edge where the tunnel cover meets the carpet / sill? If yes, the C-clamp approach is your Plan B.
