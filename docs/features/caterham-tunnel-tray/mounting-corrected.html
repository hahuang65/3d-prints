What actually works now

With the dash out of reach, the tray has to be secured entirely to the tunnel itself. Here are the real options, re-ranked:

🥇 Tunnel-edge C-clamp Best if edges are accessible

The tray has downward flanges that wrap around the tunnel cover's side edges, tightened with M4 screws. No adhesive, no dash needed, pure mechanical retention.

You need to check: Are the tunnel cover's side edges visible/exposed? Or are they hidden under carpet or sill trim?

No adhesive Fully reversible Survives track heat

🥇 Dual Lock + low-profile Always works

3M Dual Lock on the tray floor, pressed onto the tunnel. The key insight: if the tray walls are kept low (15–20 mm) with a chamfered/tapered front edge, knees slide over it rather than hooking under it — dramatically reducing peel risk.

The lid (Option B) also acts as a smooth, sloped surface that deflects knees instead of catching them. A front lip that curves upward into the void under the dash could also serve as a visual/mechanical "stop" if someone's knee does push the tray rearward.

Simple print Shear is strong Peel mitigated by low profile

Hybrid: Dual Lock + side-wall adhesive Extra grip

The tray isn't just a flat dish — it has side walls that extend down the sides of the tunnel, with additional Dual Lock or VHB patches on the vertical faces. Peel loads on the top surface become shear loads on the side patches.

More surface area Converts peel → shear

Hook-and-stop: use the dash overhang differently If geometry allows

Instead of a tongue under the dash, the tray has a vertical front wall that rises up into the void. If the tray is bumped upward (peel event), the front wall contacts the underside of the dash overhang before the Dual Lock peels. The dash acts as a rotation stop.

This only works if (a) the tray sits far enough forward that its front wall is under the overhang, and (b) the overhang is low enough that the wall reaches it without the tray being comically tall.
