01 The Space

The S3 transmission tunnel tapers from the bulkhead rearward. At the forward end — just behind the dash — it's at its narrowest and sits between the driver's and passenger's knees. Understanding the geometry here is the first design constraint.

02 Clearances & Conflicts

The forward tunnel zone is tight. These are the things the tray must not interfere with.

Must clear
  • Gear shifter: The lever sweeps rearward from its pivot. The tray must end before the pivot, or notch around it.
  • Dashboard underside: The dash wraps down and rearward. The tray slides under this overhang — measure the gap.
  • Heater controls / wiring: If the car has a heater, the control cables and fan unit live under the dash centre.
  • Knee clearance: Driver's right knee and passenger's left knee bracket the tunnel. The tray shouldn't add width that intrudes.
  • Handbrake: On S3 cars this is often on the left sill or tunnel-side. Verify position.
  • Harness slots: If you have aftermarket seats with 6-point harnesses, the tunnel-side straps route around the tunnel.
Environmental
  • Heat: The transmission tunnel gets hot — especially the 420R's 2.0L Duratec + gearbox. ASA or PETG (as in your other designs) is the right call. No PLA.
  • Vibration: Live axle + cycle wings = a lot of vibration. Items in the tray will rattle. Consider a lined / grippy floor or a lid.
  • Sun exposure: If the tray peeks out from under the dash, it'll see direct sun. UV-stable materials matter.
  • Water / damp: Caterhams are not weather sealed. The tray should drain or have raised edges so a spilled drink doesn't pool.

03 Design Directions

Three distinct approaches, each with a different complexity-to-usefulness tradeoff.

Option A · Simple Tray

Flat-bottomed dish, contoured to the tunnel profile.

  • Single-piece, no moving parts
  • Low side walls (15–25 mm) to minimise knee interference
  • Grippy floor pattern (chequered / ribs) to stop sliding
  • Attaches via industrial-strength dual-lock (3M) or velcro
  • Shallow phone/keys/glasses tray — no lid
Print: 1 piece No hardware Supports? maybe
Option B · Tray with Lid

Hinged or sliding lid keeps items contained.

  • Two-piece: tray base + lid (sliding dovetail or hinge)
  • Lid prevents items flying out during spirited driving
  • Optional latch (friction-fit or magnetic catch)
  • Deeper tray (30–40 mm) for wallet, phone, tool roll
  • Lid doubles as an arm-rest pad if flush with the tunnel
Print: 2 pieces Sliding joint No hardware
Option C · Modular System

Baseplate + interchangeable inserts.

  • Baseplate permanently mounted to the tunnel (screws or adhesive)
  • Swappable inserts: cup holder, phone mount, tray, tool caddy
  • Dovetail / T-track interface between base and inserts
  • Wiring pass-through in the base for USB charger integration
  • Over-engineered but future-proof

04 Mounting Strategies

The tunnel cover is usually powder-coated aluminium or gel-coated fibre glass. Whatever you choose needs to hold under cornering loads without damaging the tunnel.

Adhesive / Dual Lock
  • 3M™ Dual Lock™ (SJ3550 series) — the heavy-duty mushroom-head fastener. Holds ~5 kg per 25×25 mm patch in shear. Works on painted aluminium and gel coat.
  • VHB tape (3M 4950) — permanent bond. Good for a baseplate. Requires isopropyl wipe + 24 h cure.
  • Best for: Options A and B. Zero modification to the car. Removable.
  • Watch out: Gel coat can delaminate if you pull too hard. Warm the tape before removal.
Recommendation
Dual lock corners on the tray floor. The shear load during cornering is handled by the large surface area, not peel.
Mechanical / Clamp
  • Screw into tunnel: Only works if you can access the underside (centre tunnel panel often removable). M4 or M5 button-head screws into rivnuts or self-tappers.
  • Clamp around tunnel flanges: Some tunnels have a lip where the cover meets the floor. A C-channel clamp could grip that edge without drilling.
  • Dash-bracket: A tab that reaches forward and bolts to the underside of the dash frame (if accessible).
  • Best for: Option C (permanent baseplate) or high-load items.
  • Watch out: Drilling into the tunnel can let in water or fumes. Seal any holes.

Tunnel contour — measurement method

The tunnel top may be flat, crowned, or a compound radius. Here's how to capture it for the OpenSCAD model:

  1. Profile gauge — press a contour gauge against the tunnel top to capture the cross-section, then trace onto paper.
  2. Cardboard template — notch a piece of corrugated cardboard to the tunnel width, slide it forward until it contacts the dash. Trace the profile.
  3. Calipers at stations — measure width at 20 mm intervals along the tunnel length. Plot the taper.
  4. Photogrammetry — take a side-by-side photo with a scale reference; trace in CAD.

07 Decisions Needed

Before diving into OpenSCAD, we need to converge on a few key choices.

To decide now
  • Which design direction? Simple tray (A), lid (B), or modular (C)?
  • What's the primary use? Daily phone/keys, or track-day tool caddy?
  • Do you want a phone charge passthrough? (USB-C hole or Qi pad pocket)
  • Colour? Match the tunnel (black/grey) or contrast (raw ASA, red accent)?
  • Surface finish? Textured top (cosmetic) vs smooth print face-down?
Measurements to take
  • Tunnel width at the dash face (front) and at the shifter (rear)
  • Tunnel crown / profile cross-section
  • Dash overhang depth and ground clearance
  • Shifter pivot location relative to dash face
  • Handbrake location (confirm it's not on the tunnel)
  • Knee clearance — sit in the car, mark where your knees land
  • Heater / wiring obstruction under the dash centre
